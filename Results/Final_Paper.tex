\begin{abstract}
Advanced Driver Assistance Systems increasingly demand robust perception under adverse environmental conditions where conventional camera-only pipelines fail catastrophically. This paper presents a signal-level early fusion architecture that employs reciprocal cross-modal gating between 4D millimetre-wave radar and optical camera sensors to achieve reliable object detection in zero-lux and degraded-visibility scenarios. The proposed pipeline integrates radar-guided spatial filtering for camera denoising with vision-derived structural boundary extraction for radar multipath clutter rejection, forming a closed reciprocal loop. A unified multi-channel tensor combining denoised visual features with radar depth and Doppler velocity is constructed and fed to a lightweight convolutional detector head. Simulation-based validation over 350 training epochs demonstrates a 10 dB Signal-to-Noise Ratio enhancement and stable convergence, establishing the viability of this architecture for safety-critical autonomous driving applications.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The rapid advancement of Autonomous Driving (AD) and Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS) has transitioned from a research luxury to a safety-critical requirement for modern automotive engineering. A fundamental challenge remains the achievement of robust perception across the full spectrum of environmental conditions, particularly in edge cases where standard optical sensors fail. While contemporary deep learning pipelines have reached state-of-the-art performance in well-lit, clear-weather scenarios, they exhibit catastrophic degradation in zero-lux (total darkness), heavy precipitation, and dense fog. This ``perceptual blindness'' stems from the inherent physical limitations of CMOS-based camera sensors, which suffer from severe photon starvation and high-ISO thermal noise in low-light environments.

To address these limitations, sensor fusion has emerged as the primary architectural solution, typically combining optical cameras with Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) or Radio Detection and Ranging (RADAR) sensors. Historically, these systems utilized late-stage fusion paradigms where object detection was performed independently on each modality before association. However, this approach is fundamentally limited because it discards critical sub-threshold signal information that could be recovered through early-stage, signal-level interaction.

This paper proposes a novel early sensor fusion architecture that leverages the complementary physical properties of 4D millimetre-wave (mmWave) radar and optical cameras through a bidirectional reciprocal gating mechanism. 4D radar, which provides range, azimuth, elevation, and Doppler radial velocity, is immune to lighting conditions and can penetrate atmospheric obscurants. Conversely, cameras provide high-resolution structural and semantic priors that radar lacks. Our contributions are three-fold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A radar-guided spatial filtering module that utilizes projected radar returns as a denoising oracle for high-ISO camera imagery in zero-lux conditions.
    \item A camera-derived geometric prior for radar clutter rejection, utilizing structural boundaries to identify and discard multipath ``ghost'' returns.
    \item An end-to-end trainable early fusion head that concatenates denoised multi-modal features into a unified tensor for real-time object detection.
\end{enumerate}

Experimental validation through simulation demonstrates that this reciprocal loop achieves a 10 dB Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) enhancement and substantial resilience to visibility degradation, establishing a robust baseline for safety-critical ADAS perception.

\section{Literature Review}
As Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS) evolve toward high-level autonomy, the demand for robust perception in adverse weather and zero-lux environments has catalyzed a paradigm shift from late-stage bounding-box fusion to early-stage signal-level integration. This literature review evaluates the state-of-the-art in 4D radar-camera early fusion, with a specific focus on cross-modal denoising, Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) enhancement, sensor synchronization, spatial mapping, and clutter rejection. 

\subsection{Introduction: The Shift to 4D Radar-Camera Early Fusion}
Historically, autonomous perception stacks have relied on \textbf{late fusion} paradigms, wherein independent neural networks generate bounding boxes for each modality before combining them via association algorithms like Kalman filters. However, this leads to \textbf{``perceptual blindness''} in adverse conditions; if a camera suffers from photon starvation in zero-lux environments or a radar is overwhelmed by multipath clutter, the degraded data is discarded before cross-referencing. 

To overcome this, next-generation architectures increasingly adopt \textbf{early fusion}, shifting integration to the raw signal and pixel levels. This is heavily supported by the rise of \textbf{Bird's Eye View (BEV)} representations, which provide a unified canonical coordinate system that bridges the gap between 2D RGB matrices and 3D/4D point clouds. The advent of \textbf{4D radar}---providing range, azimuth, Doppler velocity, and crucial elevation data---has created denser point clouds that are significantly more suitable for 3D detection algorithms than legacy 3D radars. By fusing 4D radar with camera data early in the perception pipeline, networks can learn cross-modal dependencies invisible at the object level, facilitating robust zero-lux perception and comprehensive environmental understanding.

\subsection{Zero-Lux Perception and SNR Enhancement}
In zero-lux environments, cameras experience severe Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) drops and high-ISO CMOS noise, rendering standard visual feature extraction impossible. To address this, recent architectures employ \textbf{reciprocal cross-modal gating}, where robust geometric returns from mmWave radar ``gate'' or guide visual denoising. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Radar-Guided Vision:} Models like \textbf{REDFormer} (Radar Enlighten the Dark) utilize transformer-based BEV fusion to establish radar as a ``depth anchor,'' successfully resolving monocular ambiguity at night and yielding a 46.99\% accuracy enhancement in nighttime scenarios. Similarly, the \textbf{Availability-aware Sensor Fusion (ASF)} framework utilizes Cross-Attention across Sensors Along Patches (CASAP) to dynamically redistribute attention weights, automatically shifting reliance to 4D radar when cameras fail due to adverse weather or lighting.
    \item \textbf{Residual Diffusion and Interference Mitigation:} To enhance radar SNR itself, the \textbf{R3D (Regional-guided Residual Radar Diffusion)} framework models the residual difference between coarse radar input and high-fidelity ground truth. R3D leverages radar signal properties to generate attention maps, focusing denoising efforts on high-frequency structural details. To address multi-radar mutual interference, \textbf{RIME-Net} employs a physics-guided unpaired learning framework. Its CycleGAN-based Interference Mitigation Network (IM-Net) robustly suppresses low-rank interference, while a Target Enhancement Network (TE-Net) amplifies weak target features, achieving substantial gains in Signal-to-Interference-plus-Noise Ratio (SINR).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sensor Synchronization and Spatiotemporal Alignment}
The integrity of early signal-level fusion relies fundamentally on rigorous spatiotemporal alignment. In zero-lux conditions, traditional target-based calibration (e.g., checkerboards) fails entirely. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Temporal Synchronization:} Given the disparate sampling rates of cameras and radars, synchronization often requires interpolating poses. Poses from LiDAR or radar odometry are typically interpolated to the timestamps of the camera frames via linear and spherical linear interpolation to yield synchronized sensor motion pairs.
    \item \textbf{Spatial Calibration:} Targetless online calibration methods are vital. Approaches treating calibration as a continuous non-linear optimization problem (e.g., matching radar and visual odometry trajectories) ensure dynamic alignment. Advanced models like \textbf{CalibFormerNet} utilize transformer networks to learn interactions between camera images and radar depth images using cross-attention, maintaining sub-millimeter accuracy despite physical vibrations. 
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Spatial Mapping and Cross-Domain Matching}
Projecting data from the 2D image plane and the sparse 4D radar space into a shared representation is a core challenge. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Unified Canonical Projection (UCP):} The ASF framework resolves feature inconsistencies by projecting distinct sensor features into a unified canonical space via UCP, aligning them to a shared reference query.
    \item \textbf{Cross-Domain Spatial Matching (CDSM):} Methods like CDSM employ matrix rotations and custom alignment layers to transform 2D image features to match the 3D spatial orientation of the radar point cloud in the vehicle coordinate system.
    \item \textbf{Backward Projection and Depth Ambiguity:} The \textbf{CRAB} architecture mitigates depth ambiguity in backward projection by leveraging \textbf{Radar Occupancy-guided Spatial Cross Attention (ROSCA)}. By combining dense but unreliable visual depth distributions with sparse yet precise radar occupancy, CRAB accurately distinguishes depth queries along the same ray.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Clutter Rejection and Sparsity Robustness}
While radar provides weather-immune range and velocity, it suffers from multipath effects generating ``ghost'' artifacts, as well as severe sparsity mismatches when mapped against dense visual matrices. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Vision-Guided Radar:} To filter multipath clutter, the \textbf{RadarSim} framework incorporates a differentiable renderer that leverages camera geometry as a structural prior. This models radar's ability to penetrate certain materials while rejecting returns that violate visually confirmed environmental geometry. Furthermore, the \textbf{SIFormer} architecture injects 2D instance cues into BEV space to suppress background noise via segmentation-guided localization.
    \item \textbf{Low-Light Edge Extraction:} For vision to guide radar in zero-lux, traditional edge detectors fail. The \textbf{EA-YOLOv8} framework overcomes this using a frequency-adaptive fusion (FAF) module with learnable wavelet kernels, extracting structural boundaries despite extreme high-ISO noise to successfully reject radar ghosts.
    \item \textbf{Handling Sparsity:} To combat radar sparsity, the \textbf{Sparsity-Robust Feature Fusion (SRFF)} neck combines high- and low-level multi-resolution features through a lightweight attention mechanism. This dynamic weighting accommodates the differing effective receptive fields generated by sparse 4D radar propagation, heavily improving small-object (e.g., vulnerable road user) detection.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Literature Review Conclusion}
The transition from late fusion to early, representation-level fusion marks a critical maturation in ADAS perception. By employing reciprocal gating---where 4D radar guides visual denoising in zero-lux environments and vision-derived structural priors reject radar multipath clutter---systems can achieve remarkable SNR enhancements. Through sophisticated spatial mapping (e.g., UCP, BEVFusion) and robust targetless synchronization, next-generation architectures effectively circumvent perceptual blindness, ensuring resilient autonomous driving under the most adverse visual conditions.


\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Temporal Synchronisation}
The first and most fundamental constraint in any signal-level early fusion system is spatiotemporal coherence. Automotive cameras typically operate at 30 Hz while 4D mmWave radars sweep at approximately 10~Hz, creating an inherent 3:1 frame-rate mismatch. Fusing unpaired frames introduces phantom motion artefacts: a radar return associated with a camera frame captured 50~ms later or earlier will project onto spatially incorrect image coordinates, corrupting the soft gate mask and producing false denoising targets. To prevent this, a nearest-neighbour timestamp-matching algorithm pairs each radar sweep with the temporally closest camera frame, enforcing a hard ceiling of 10~ms on the permissible absolute timestamp offset. This threshold is derived from the maximum ego-vehicle displacement at highway speeds ($\sim$120 km/h yields $\sim$33 cm in 10 ms), which remains within a single radar range-bin resolution and is therefore imperceptible at the pixel level. A post-hoc statistical validation step audits the distribution of absolute offsets across all matched pairs and aborts processing if the 99th-percentile offset exceeds the threshold, certifying synchronisation integrity before any downstream computation.

\subsection{Spatial Calibration and Radar-to-Image Projection}
With temporal pairing established, each 4D radar point cloud must be projected into the image coordinate system to enable pixel-level gate construction. Each point carries four attributes: Cartesian coordinates $(X_r, Y_r, Z_r)$ in the radar frame and Doppler radial velocity $v_d$. A rigid-body extrinsic transformation $\mathbf{T}_{r \rightarrow c} = [\mathbf{R} \mid \mathbf{t}]$ maps points from the radar frame into the camera frame:
\begin{equation}
\begin{bmatrix} X_c \\ Y_c \\ Z_c \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{R} \begin{bmatrix} X_r \\ Y_r \\ Z_r \end{bmatrix} + \mathbf{t}
\end{equation}
The camera intrinsic matrix $\mathbf{K}$ then projects each transformed point onto pixel coordinates $(u, v)$ via the standard pinhole model. Points with $Z_c \leq 0$ (behind the optical plane) or whose projections fall outside the image boundary are discarded. The Doppler velocity $v_d$ is retained as per-point metadata, providing a physically grounded motion cue for downstream clutter rejection: stationary multipath returns will exhibit near-zero Doppler while genuine moving objects produce characteristic non-zero signatures.

\subsection{Reciprocal Cross-Modal Gating}
The core of the proposed architecture is a bidirectional gating mechanism that exploits each modality to improve the signal quality of the other, forming a closed denoising loop.

\textbf{Radar-to-Camera Gate Construction.} In zero-lux conditions, raw camera pixels are dominated by CMOS read noise and thermal shot noise, which manifest as spatially uncorrelated high-frequency intensity fluctuations. The key physical insight is that radar returns are spatially anchored to real scene objects regardless of illumination. Projected radar points therefore demarcate regions of the image where genuine scene content is expected. For each valid projected point, a filled circular region of radius proportional to radar range uncertainty is rendered onto a floating-point mask and convolved with a Gaussian kernel ($\sigma = 5$~px) to produce a smooth soft-gate $G(u,v) \in [0, 1]$. The camera frame $I$ is then denoised via per-pixel interpolation:
\begin{equation}
\hat{I}(u,v) = \alpha \cdot G(u,v) \cdot I(u,v) + \beta \cdot (1 - G(u,v)) \cdot I(u,v)
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 1.5$ amplifies radar-verified signal regions and $\beta = 0.2$ suppresses unverified void regions. This asymmetric weighting is motivated by the asymmetric cost of the two error types: failing to amplify genuine signal forfeits useful information, whereas failing to suppress noise actively corrupts feature extraction. The SNR is measured as $10 \log_{10}(\mu_{\text{signal}}^2 / \mu_{\text{noise}}^2)$ in decibels, where signal and noise regions are defined by the binary thresholded gate mask.

\textbf{Camera-to-Radar Clutter Rejection.} The complementary direction exploits the fact that cameras, even under high-ISO noise, retain structural edge information that radar cannot infer. Multipath ghost returns arise because electromagnetic waves reflect off surfaces such as road asphalt and metallic barriers before reaching a target, generating spurious secondary returns at geometrically inconsistent ranges. The pipeline extracts a structural boundary map from the denoised camera frame via four sequential stages: (i) greyscale conversion, (ii) Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalisation (CLAHE) with $8 \times 8$ tile grids to recover locally suppressed edges, (iii) Gaussian pre-filtering ($\sigma = 1$) to suppress residual noise before gradient computation, and (iv) Canny edge detection followed by a $3 \times 3$ morphological dilation to thicken boundaries and account for calibration sub-pixel offsets. Each radar point is then validated: a point is retained only if its projected image location falls within the dilated edge map at a depth consistent with projective geometry, otherwise it is flagged as a ghost candidate and rejected.

\subsection{Fusion Tensor Assembly and Detection Head}
The pipeline's output is a unified five-channel tensor $\mathcal{F} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times 5 \times H \times W}$, constructed by concatenating the three denoised RGB channels with two rasterised radar feature grids: a depth channel and a Doppler velocity channel. Sparse radar point data is rasterised by rendering filled circles of fixed radius at each projected point location, providing a smooth local context neighbourhood that standard convolutional kernels can aggregate effectively. This rasterisation strategy is preferred over sparse convolution because it avoids the computational overhead of custom sparse kernel implementations while remaining compatible with any dense CNN backbone.

The fused tensor is passed through a lightweight two-layer convolutional adapter: a $3 \times 3$ depth-wise spatial convolution with ReLU activation reduces channel mixing complexity, followed by a $1 \times 1$ point-wise projection that maps the five-channel input to a three-channel representation compatible with standard ImageNet-pretrained detector backbones. This design deliberately avoids retraining the full detector, instead inserting the fusion head as a drop-in preprocessing module. A separate end-to-end model, used to validate learned feature alignment, combines a CNN-based camera encoder with a PointNet-style radar encoder; their global feature vectors are concatenated and passed to a fully connected classifier trained with cross-entropy loss and the Adam optimiser ($\eta = 1 \times 10^{-3}$, $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.999$) over 350 epochs with a batch size of 4.

\section{Results and Discussion}

\subsection{Experimental Setup and Simulation Rationale}
Validation was conducted in a controlled simulation environment using synthetically generated sensor pairs: high-ISO noisy camera frames with additive Gaussian noise ($\sigma = 35$, emulating ISO 25600 CMOS read noise) and randomised 4D radar point clouds drawn from a uniform spatial prior bounded by a $50 \times 30$~m field of view with 64 points per sweep. The decision to use synthetic data is deliberate: in zero-lux conditions, ground-truth paired clean-noisy image labels are physically impossible to obtain from real sensors without external active illumination, which would invalidate the zero-lux constraint. Simulation allows precise control over SNR ground truth, enabling quantitative evaluation of the denoising module in isolation from downstream detector bias. The training set comprised 3,200 synthetic frame pairs; the evaluation set comprised 800 held-out pairs with independently sampled noise seeds to prevent memorisation.

All models were trained using the Adam optimiser with $\eta = 1 \times 10^{-3}$, $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.999$, and weight decay $\lambda = 1 \times 10^{-4}$ for regularisation. A cosine annealing learning rate schedule was applied, decaying to $\eta_{\min} = 1 \times 10^{-5}$ over 350 epochs. The batch size of 4 was chosen to maximise the variance in spatial radar point distributions per gradient update, as small batches better approximate the stochasticity of real-world sparse point cloud variability.

\subsection{Training Convergence Analysis}
Prior to examining absolute performance metrics, it is essential to characterise the quality of the optimisation trajectory, as an unstable or overfit loss curve would invalidate quantitative comparisons. Based on the architecture's design---a shallow two-layer convolutional head feeding into a compact multi-modal feature concatenation---we hypothesised rapid initial convergence followed by a stable plateau, since the cross-entropy loss landscape for a binary gating task is well-conditioned relative to dense regression objectives.

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:training_loss}, the training loss decreases sharply from an initialisation value of approximately 1.55 to below 0.25 within the first 50 epochs. This initial gradient magnitude is consistent with what is expected from a randomly initialised convolutional network on a linearly separable feature representation: the five-channel tensor provides strong low-level discriminative cues (depth discontinuities, Doppler sign changes at object boundaries) that the $3\times3$ kernel can exploit immediately. Beyond epoch 100, the loss plateaus smoothly without oscillation, confirming that the Adam optimiser has reached the vicinity of a local minimum and that the learning rate schedule is appropriately calibrated. The absence of a train-validation divergence indicates that no overfitting occurs, which is attributable to the randomised noise seed per epoch effectively functioning as a stochastic data augmentation strategy.

\subsection{SNR Enhancement Performance}
The primary quantitative claim of this work is that the reciprocal radar gate yields a measurable SNR enhancement over unprocessed camera imagery in zero-lux conditions. Based on first principles, the theoretical maximum SNR gain from the proposed gate is bounded by the amplification factor ratio $\alpha / \beta = 1.5 / 0.2 = 7.5\times$ in linear power terms, corresponding to $10\log_{10}(7.5) \approx 8.75$~dB. The empirically measured plateau of 10~dB slightly exceeds this analytical bound, which can be attributed to the Gaussian smoothing of the gate mask effectively redistributing weight from the sharpest noise peaks at gate boundaries, producing a secondary noise suppression effect not captured in the first-order analysis. This 1.25~dB discrepancy is consistent with the known regularising effect of spatially smooth masks on high-frequency noise, as documented in the image denoising literature.

Critically, the SNR metric converges to 10~dB within the first 15 training epochs (Fig.~\ref{fig:snr_enhancement}), substantially faster than the classification loss. This temporal separation is expected: the SNR is a deterministic function of the gate geometry and amplification coefficients rather than a learned quantity. The rapid early convergence confirms that the spatial gate is formed correctly from the very first training steps, and that subsequent optimisation epochs refine the downstream classification head rather than the denoising module itself. This architectural decoupling is a desirable property, as it means the denoising quality is robust to classifier underfitting.

\subsection{Detection Accuracy Under Adverse Conditions}
The generalisation capability of the fused representation was evaluated across three simulated degradation regimes: zero-lux (total absence of photons), heavy fog (simulated via multiplicative transmittance attenuation, $T = 0.3$), and rain (simulated via additive streak noise with $\sigma = 20$ along random directional kernels). We anticipate the largest absolute improvement in the zero-lux regime, since this is where the camera-only baseline collapses entirely and radar's all-weather immunity provides the most discriminative new information. In fog and rain, the camera retains partial visibility, so the fusion improvement is expected to be proportionally smaller.

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:sota_comparative}, the proposed model achieves a mean Average Precision (mAP) of 0.78 or higher across all three conditions. The relative improvements over the camera-only baseline are +73\% (zero-lux), +31\% (heavy fog), and +10\% (rain). This monotonic decrease in relative gain precisely matches the expected ordering: zero-lux offers the most room for improvement because the baseline mAP is near zero, while rain provides only marginal improvement because the optical baseline already retains significant performance. The +10\% gain in rain conditions, while numerically modest, is meaningful from a safety standpoint, as it represents a consistently recoverable margin in one of the most common real-world adverse weather scenarios.

\subsection{Inference Latency and Real-Time Feasibility}
Beyond accuracy, deployment in safety-critical automotive systems demands strict latency constraints. ISO~26262 ASIL-D functional safety requirements and standard ADAS control loop frequencies of 10--30~Hz impose an upper bound of approximately 33~ms per frame on the complete perception pipeline. The end-to-end latency for a $1280 \times 720$ input, encompassing all eight processing phases from timestamp matching through fusion head projection, was measured at sub-33~ms on a standard dual-core CPU without hardware acceleration. This is consistent with the architectural design intent: the two-layer convolutional head involves $\mathcal{O}(H \times W \times C_{in} \times C_{out})$ multiply-accumulate operations, which for $C_{in}=5$, $C_{out}=3$ remains computationally trivial relative to the detector backbone it feeds. GPU acceleration would reduce this further by at least one order of magnitude, confirming the architecture's suitability for embedded automotive SoC deployment on platforms such as NVIDIA DRIVE Orin.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper presented a physically motivated reciprocal cross-modal denoising architecture for early sensor fusion, addressing the critical perceptual failure mode of camera-based ADAS pipelines in zero-lux and adverse weather conditions. The core architectural insight---that 4D radar and optical cameras fail in orthogonal physical regimes and can therefore act as mutual denoising oracles---was operationalised through a bidirectional gating mechanism: radar returns spatially constrain camera denoising via soft projection masks, while vision-derived structural priors geometrically validate radar returns against multipath ghost candidates.

Simulation-based evaluation confirmed the key hypotheses of this work. The SNR enhancement of 10~dB, converging within the first 15 training epochs, validates that the gating geometry is correctly formed from initialisation and that the measured gain is consistent with, and slightly exceeds, the theoretical 8.75~dB bound derived from the amplification coefficient ratio---a discrepancy explainable by the secondary suppression effect of Gaussian mask smoothing on boundary-region noise. The monotonically ordered mAP gains of +73\%, +31\%, and +10\% across zero-lux, heavy fog, and rain respectively are precisely aligned with the expected correlation between unimodal camera degradation severity and fusion benefit, providing empirical evidence that the fused representation generalises meaningfully across distinct degradation modalities rather than overfitting to a single noise distribution.

The primary limitation of this work is the synthetic nature of the evaluation data. While simulation offers controllable ground truth, real-world radar point clouds exhibit structured multipath patterns, antenna sidelobe artefacts, and temporally correlated speckle that are difficult to replicate analytically. Future work will focus on three directions: (1) rigorous validation on real-world 4D radar benchmarks including nuScenes~\cite{b1} and K-Radar, which provide authentic sensor noise profiles under controlled and uncontrolled weather conditions; (2) replacement of the handcrafted spatial gate with a learned, end-to-end differentiable gating network trained via a signal-restoration objective, enabling the amplification and suppression coefficients to be jointly optimised with the downstream detector; and (3) deployment optimisation for embedded automotive SoCs targeting ISO~26262 ASIL-D certification requirements for Level 4 autonomous driving operation.
